\section{Background and Related Work}

\fakepar{LoRaWAN for IoT Connectivity}
LoRaWAN has emerged as a widely used low-power wide-area networking technology for Internet-of-Things (IoT) deployments that require long range, low energy consumption, and low infrastructure cost~\cite{lorawan_spec,lorawan_architecture}. In a typical LoRaWAN deployment, battery-powered end devices transmit uplink packets that may be received by multiple gateways within radio range. These gateways forward the packets over IP backhaul to a network server, which performs tasks such as packet deduplication, device management, and delivery to application backends~\cite{lorawan_spec,lorawan_backend,ttn_architecture}. This architecture improves reliability because successful reception does not depend on any single gateway. However, it also introduces redundancy by design: a single uplink may be forwarded multiple times through the backhaul before duplicate copies are removed at the backend~\cite{lorawan_spec,lorawan_backend}. Prior studies on LoRa and LoRaWAN scalability further show that dense reception and shared infrastructure can create significant capacity pressure as deployments grow~\cite{bor_lora_scale16,georgiou_lora_scale17}.

\fakepar{Why Federated IoT Networks Matter}
As IoT deployments scale across large geographic regions, a federated network model becomes increasingly attractive. Instead of relying on a single centrally deployed operator infrastructure, federated IoT networks allow many independently deployed gateways or access points to contribute connectivity into a shared service fabric. This model improves scalability, coverage, and deployment incentives, especially in environments where infrastructure density varies across neighborhoods or cities. At the same time, federation shifts some challenges from the wireless edge into the network core. Since independently deployed gateways may all forward traffic toward shared backend services, duplicate receptions are no longer merely a backend software issue; they also consume shared access and aggregation bandwidth. As a result, efficient backhaul handling becomes an important systems problem in federated IoT infrastructure~\cite{lorawan_architecture,helium_overview,helium_imc21,bor_lora_scale16,georgiou_lora_scale17}.

\fakepar{Helium as a Federated LoRaWAN Deployment}
Helium is a prominent example of a federated LoRaWAN-style network, in which connectivity is provided not by a single centrally deployed operator infrastructure, but by many independently operated community hotspots~\cite{helium_overview,helium_website,helium_imc21}. As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:heliumarch}, battery-powered end devices transmit LoRa uplinks that may be received by one or more nearby hotspots. Each hotspot acts as a gateway: it demodulates the wireless packet, encapsulates it into standard IP traffic, and forwards it over the backhaul towards cloud~\cite{lorawan_backend,ttn_architecture}. This federated architecture is attractive because it improves coverage and scalability while reducing the need for a single entity to deploy and maintain the entire access infrastructure.

However, the same architecture also creates a major inefficiency. Since LoRaWAN uplinks are broadcast in nature, the same device transmission may be heard by multiple hotspots within range~\cite{lorawan_spec,lorawan_architecture,bor_lora_scale16,georgiou_lora_scale17}. In current deployments, each hotspot independently forwards its received copy of the packet toward the backend, even though these copies correspond to the same logical uplink~\cite{lorawan_backend,helium_imc21}. As illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:redundancy}, the backend eventually performs deduplication and preserves correctness at the application layer, but by that point the redundant copies have already consumed backhaul bandwidth. This wastes aggregation capacity and becomes increasingly costly in dense deployments where multiple hotspots frequently hear the same transmission.

Beyond simple delivery, Helium also imposes an additional requirement: attribution. Incentive and accounting mechanisms such as proof-of-coverage and data-transfer rewards depend on preserving information about hotspot participation in the network~\cite{helium_overview,helium_dataonly,helium_rewards,helium_imc21}. Therefore, an in-network deduplication mechanism for Helium must do more than suppress redundant traffic; it must also preserve the attribution semantics required for rewards, auditing, and tenant-specific backend processing.

\fakepar{Deduplication and In-Network Processing}
Packet deduplication is commonly performed at the backend in LoRaWAN-style systems, where multiple gateway copies of the same uplink are forwarded to a centralized service and filtered in software. This approach is simple and flexible, but it removes duplicates only after they have already consumed backhaul bandwidth and processing resources. More broadly, intermediate duplicate suppression and in-network aggregation have been explored in wireless sensor networks and edge systems to reduce communication overhead and improve efficiency~\cite{tada}. Closely related to our setting, prior work has also explored reducing duplicate LoRaWAN gateway forwarding through coding-aware forwarding strategies~\cite{alawami_lorawan_forwarding22}. However, many such approaches operate at different layers, assume more homogeneous network stacks, or do not address the constraints of shared federated IoT backhaul. In contrast, FeBEx moves duplicate suppression earlier into the network and targets Helium-style LoRaWAN deployments, where duplicate filtering must coexist with tenant-aware forwarding and hotspot attribution~\cite{tada,alawami_lorawan_forwarding22}.

\fakepar{P4 and Programmable Data Planes}
P4 enables custom packet-processing logic to be implemented directly in programmable switch data planes~\cite{p4}. Prior work has used P4 to support stateful network functions such as in-band telemetry~\cite{int}, load balancing~\cite{conga}, and in-network aggregation~\cite{netcache}. More recent work has further shown that programmable switches can support richer in-network computation primitives beyond conventional forwarding~\cite{chen_p4com21}. Together, these systems demonstrate that programmable switches can maintain bounded state, perform packet cloning, and execute application-specific forwarding decisions at line rate. FeBEx builds on the same general capabilities, but applies them to a different problem: duplicate suppression in federated IoT backhaul. In particular, it combines register-based state, match-action forwarding, and cloning to jointly support duplicate filtering, tenant steering, and proof-of-coverage-aware attribution.

\fakepar{Hash-Based Filtering}
Compact duplicate detection is often implemented using hash tables or Bloom-filter-like structures~\cite{hashtables,bloomfilters}. Storing full packet contents or performing exact-match lookup is typically too expensive for switch memory, making bounded-state hashing a more suitable approach in programmable data planes. FeBEx adopts a two-level hash design: one hash selects the register index, while a second hash serves as a verification key to reduce false matches caused by collisions~\cite{hashtables}. Compared to probabilistic filters such as Bloom filters~\cite{bloomfilters}, this design is simpler to express in P4 and aligns naturally with deterministic register lookups. The tradeoff is that correctness depends on suitable provisioning of register capacity and expiry intervals, which FeBEx explicitly evaluates.

\fakepar{IoT Backhaul and Edge Network Services}
Prior work on programmable switches has increasingly explored stateful edge services that move functionality from servers or middleboxes into the forwarding path. Examples include telemetry~\cite{int}, switch-assisted coordination~\cite{silkroad}, load-aware forwarding~\cite{conga}, and more general in-network computation~\cite{chen_p4com21}. At the same time, LoRaWAN and Helium-style infrastructures face practical backhaul challenges including redundant forwarding, bandwidth cost, and the need for accurate attribution in incentivized deployments~\cite{helium_imc21}. FeBEx sits at the intersection of these directions: it treats duplicate suppression not as a backend-only software task, but as an in-network edge service implemented in the data plane.

\fakepar{Positioning of FeBEx}
FeBEx is distinguished by the combination of four properties. First, it is motivated by Helium-style federated LoRaWAN deployments rather than generic packet filtering~\cite{helium_imc21}. Second, it embeds duplicate suppression directly in a programmable forwarding pipeline instead of relying on separate middleboxes or cloud-side processing. Third, it operates under bounded switch state using a compact register-based design with epoch-based expiry and hash-based verification~\cite{hashtables,bloomfilters}. Finally, it preserves practical deployment requirements beyond correctness alone, including multi-tenant traffic steering and attribution-aware packet cloning. Together, these aspects position FeBEx at the intersection of backhaul optimization, programmable data-plane systems, and compact in-network duplicate filtering.
