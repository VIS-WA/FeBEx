\section{Introduction}
Helium and other federated LoRaWAN deployments have emerged as an important networking model for large-scale IoT connectivity. In these systems, a large number of geographically distributed hotspots cooperatively receive uplink transmissions from end devices and forward them toward cloud services. This architecture improves reliability and coverage, since multiple hotspots may hear the same transmission even under weak-signal or partially obstructed conditions. However, it also introduces a major inefficiency: the same logical uplink may be forwarded multiple times through the backhaul before duplicate copies are removed at the backend.

This backend-centric deduplication model wastes network resources precisely because duplicate suppression occurs too late. By the time the cloud identifies two packets as duplicates, each copy has already consumed access and aggregation bandwidth. In dense urban deployments, where a single uplink may be received by several hotspots, this behavior can significantly inflate backhaul traffic, increase network cost, and reduce effective aggregation capacity. In Helium-style deployments, the problem is particularly important because the backhaul is shared across independently deployed hotspots and must additionally support accounting mechanisms such as proof-of-coverage attribution.

\fakepar{Design overview} FeBEx addresses this inefficiency by moving deduplication into the network itself. Instead of allowing all copies of an uplink packet to traverse the backhaul and be filtered only at the backend, FeBEx performs duplicate suppression directly at a P4-programmable aggregation switch as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:arch}. This architectural shift allows the first valid copy of a packet to be forwarded immediately, while later duplicates are suppressed before they consume downstream bandwidth. At the same time, the switch must preserve two practical properties required in federated deployments: correct steering of packets to tenant-specific backends, and accurate attribution of the forwarded uplink.

Realizing this functionality in the data plane is not straightforward. The switch must identify duplicates using bounded state, tolerate realistic hash collisions, expire old entries without unbounded growth, and avoid falsely suppressing distinct packets. It must also support multi-tenant forwarding policies while preserving observability of the first forwarded copy for Proof-of-Coverage workflows. FeBEx addresses these requirements through a register-based deduplication pipeline with epoch-driven expiry, longest-prefix-match tenant steering, and packet cloning for attribution.

We implement FeBEx in P4-16 on BMv2 using a P4Runtime control plane and evaluate it on a Mininet-based testbed. Our evaluation studies duplicate suppression correctness, bandwidth savings, state sizing, epoch sensitivity, scalability across different deployment sizes, and multi-tenant isolation. Across the evaluated scenarios, FeBEx reduces redundant backhaul traffic by 50--90\% while preserving correct delivery behavior and accurate accounting semantics. We make the following contributions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We design and implement FeBEx, a P4-based in-network deduplication mechanism for federated LoRaWAN backhaul aggregation.
    \item We show that FeBEx suppresses redundant uplink copies before egress, substantially reducing backhaul traffic while preserving correct packet delivery.
    \item We characterize the impact of register size, epoch interval, and network scale, and derive practical guidelines for provisioning deduplication state.
    \item We demonstrate that FeBEx preserves multi-tenant traffic isolation and supports attribution alongside duplicate suppression.
\end{itemize}
